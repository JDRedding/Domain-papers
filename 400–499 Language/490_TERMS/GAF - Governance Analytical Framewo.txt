            GAF - Governance Analytical Framework
        (Faithful to the Graduate Institute GAF, adapted)
============================================================

GAF is the governance root for representational systems. GAF
provides the rules, constraints, and analytical structure 
that ensure representational systems remain explicit, 
stable, interoperable, and non‑conflated.

GAF Scope  
GAF governs representational systems (JOT‑TERMS, SPOT‑TERMS,
IOTA) and imported data objects. GAF does not govern
computational kernels or successor‑state logic.

Governance Analytical Framework (GAF)
---
GAF is the governance root for the representational TERMS ecosystem.  
It governs representational units, representational placement, and interoperability, but does not govern computational kernels.

---

Purpose of GAF

GAF exists to:

- govern representational objects (JOT‑TERMS)  
- govern representational placement (SPOT‑TERMS)  
- govern interoperability layers (IOTA)  
- maintain identity, provenance, and revision integrity  
- enforce explicitness and non‑collapse  
- provide admissibility rules for representational claims  
- ensure modularity and separation from the RDG/RDGMFEQ kernel  

GAF is an umbrella governance layer.

1. ISSUE  (The Governance Problem)
------------------------------------------------------------
The ISSUE defines what requires governance.

In this TERMS ecosystem:

    ISSUE = Representational Governance
        • identity
        • admissibility
        • placement
        • provenance
        • interoperability

GAF Components
---
GAF consists of four major governance components:

Identity Governance (GID)  
Defines how representational units maintain stable identity.

Includes:

- Constitutional Identity (CID) rules  
- Identity preservation across revisions  
- Identity conflict detection  
- Identity lineage and inheritance  

Admissibility Governance (GAD) 
Defines when representational claims are valid.

Includes:

- Representational admissibility criteria  
- Structural admissibility criteria  
- Placement admissibility criteria  
- Interoperability admissibility (for IOTA)  

This is not RDGMFEQ admissibility.  
It is representational admissibility.

Structural Governance (GST)
Defines how representational units relate to each other.

Includes:

- placement constraints  
- neighborhood rules  
- relation declarations  
- non‑collapse constraints  
- structural consistency rules  

Provenance & Revision Governance (GPR)  
Defines how representational units evolve over time.

Includes:

- revision history  
- provenance tracking  
- authorship and lineage  
- revision conflict resolution  

Relationship to Other Frameworks
---
GAF does not govern:

- successor‑state logic  
- moderation  
- Q‑slice  
- computational admissibility  
- propagation  
- residual localization  

GAF → JOT‑TERMS  
JOT‑TERMS provides the governed representational object.

GAF governs:

- identity  
- admissibility  
- provenance  
- revision  
- structural commitments  

GAF → SPOT‑TERMS
SPOT‑TERMS provides the governed representational placement.
 
GAF governs:

- placement admissibility  
- structural constraints  
- neighborhood rules  
- provenance of placement  

GAF → IOTA
IOTA provides the interoperability layer.
 
GAF governs:

- interoperability rules  
- admissibility of external objects  
- provenance of imported mappings  
- structural placement of data object derived tokens

2. ACTORS  (Governance Participants)
------------------------------------------------------------
GAF Formal Structure

GAF is defined as:

	GAF = (GID, GAD, GST, GPR)

Where:

- GID governs identity 			[Governs IDentity]
- GAD governs admissibility  		[Governs ADmissibility]
- GST governs structure and placement   [Governs STructure]
- GPR governs provenance and revision  	[Governs PRovenance]

GAF is modular, extensible, and agnostic to the computational kernel.

Actors are entities involved in governance.

In this TERMS ecosystem:

    ACTORS = {

        JOT‑TERMS units,
        SPOT‑TERMS units,
        IOTA interface objects,
        External objects (via IOTA) 
		[Data Objects (general external objects),
		 DOT Objects (one subtype)]
        Framework authors / maintainers
    }

Actors are NOT computational units.

------------------------------------------------------------
7. INVARIANTS
------------------------------------------------------------
GAF enforces the following invariants representational frameworks:

: Primary Invariants

Explicitness:
    All governance elements must be explicit. All 
    representational claims, placements, and identities must 
    be explicit.

Traceability:
    Governance processes must be reconstructible.

Non‑collapse:
    No conflation of actors, norms, or domains. No
    representational unit may collapse domain separation or
    identity boundaries.

Stability:
    Identity + provenance persist across revisions. All changes
    must be logged and traceable through GPR.

Interoperability Integrity:
    External systems may interface (via IOTA)
    but may NOT impose governance.

: Secondary Invariants

Recoverability 
Any representational unit must be reconstructible from its
governance metadata.

Consistency 
Declared relations must not contradict structural constraints.

GAF GLOSSARY
============================================================

GAF 

Governance Analytical Framework
The governance root for the TERMS ecosystem.  
GAF governs representational systems (JOT‑TERMS, SPOT‑TERMS, IOTA) and imported Data Objects, but does not govern computational kernels.  
See: GAF

---
Issue 

(Governance Problem)  
The domain requiring governance.  
In ecosystem: Representational Governance — identity, admissibility, placement, provenance, interoperability.  

---
Actors

Entities participating in governance.  
Includes:  
- JOT‑TERMS units  
- SPOT‑TERMS units  
- IOTA interface objects  
- External Data Objects  
- Framework authors/maintainers  

---
Norms

Rules and constraints shaping governance behavior.  
Includes explicitness, non‑collapse, identity preservation, admissibility, placement constraints, provenance requirements, interoperability integrity.  

---
Process

Governance as action.  
Includes justification, placement, interoperability, revision, conflict resolution, structural negotiation.  

---
Nodal Points

Locations where governance interactions concentrate.  
Includes JOT signatures, SPOT placements, IOTA mappings, GAF admissibility declarations.  

---
GID 

Identity Governance
Rules for identity stability, lineage, conflict detection, and revision‑safe identity preservation.  
See: Identity Governance

---
GAD 

Admissibility Governance
Rules determining when representational claims are valid.  
Includes representational, structural, placement, and interoperability admissibility.  
See: Admissibility Governance

---
GST 

Structural Governance
Rules governing structural relations, placement constraints, neighborhood rules, non‑collapse, and structural consistency.  
See: Structural Governance

---
GPR 

Provenance & Revision Governance
Rules governing revision history, provenance tracking, authorship, lineage, and revision conflict resolution.  
See: Provenance Governance

---
Data Objects

Any external object entering the ecosystem.  
DOT is now just **one subtype** of Data Objects.  
Data Objects are imported through IOTA and governed by GAF.  

---
IOTA — Interoperable Object Token Architecture
  
The universal import layer for Data Objects.  
IOTA translates external objects into TERMS tokens, constructs signatures, applies GAF admissibility, and produces JOT‑TERMS + SPOT‑TERMS outputs.  

---
JOT‑TERMS 

Justified Object Token  
Governed representational object.  
Built on TERMS, governed by GAF.  
Includes mutual mapping, constitutional signature, and justification.  

---
SPOT‑TERMS 

Structured Point of Tokenization**  
Governed representational placement.  
Defines locus, layer, neighborhood, relations, constraints, admissibility, and provenance of placement.  

---
TERMS 

Token Substrate
Neutral representational foundation.  
Provides thing‑tokens (⧈), representation‑tokens (⧉), and the mapping triad (↦, ↤, ↮).  

---
Prime Kernels 

Computational substrate.  
Includes RDG + RDGMFEQ, successor‑state logic, SID/PED/Q moderation, Q‑slice, computational admissibility, propagation, and residual localization.  
Not governed by GAF.  

---
Explicitness

 (Invariant)  
All governance elements must be explicit — claims, placements, identities, admissibility, provenance.

---
Traceability 

(Invariant)
Governance processes must be reconstructible from metadata.

---
Non‑collapse 

(Invariant)
No conflation of actors, norms, domains, or representational identities.

---
Stability

(Invariant)
Identity and provenance persist across revisions.

---
Interoperability Integrity
 
(Invariant)
External systems may interface via IOTA but may not impose governance.

---
Recoverability 

(Secondary Invariant)  
Representational units must be reconstructible from governance metadata.

---
Consistency 

(Secondary Invariant) 
Declared relations must not contradict structural constraints.
